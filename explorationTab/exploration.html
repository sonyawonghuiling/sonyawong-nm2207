DATA STORY

According to World Data.info, the population of Singapore had grown from 1.65 million in 1960 to 5.45 million in 2021. As one of the smallest countries in the world with a land area of 728km2, we are also one of the most densely populated countries with approximately 8000 people per square kilometre. Not only that, but Singapore has had an accelerating growth rate in its population over the last 40 years. Even with this overarching threat of overcrowding and overpopulation, the Singapore government still encourages a high influx of immigrants.

Can Singapore continue to accommodate such a growth in the coming future while land and housing remain scarce? Let's take a deeper look behind the population and demographics for a clue.


TIMELAPSE OF SINGAPORE'S SATELITTE IMAGES

Press the Play button on the left-hand side of the white bar below to see a timelapse of Singapore's satellite images over 40 years. Click the '+' sign on the right to zoom into the map. Refresh the page if the timelapse is not showing!

You can see how Singapore has undergone heavy urban development and even land reclamation processes. Our current land area stands at approximately 279.6 square miles (724.2 square kilometers). The government aims to expand our land area by an additional 8% to 300 square miles by 2030. While this means that we can theoretically just create more land if we ever run out, this plan is not as feasible as one might think. Land reclamation is both expensive and difficult, no surprise there. The process is arduous and involves an unimaginable amount of purchased sand. Not to mention, a large team of labourers and special equipment are needed for such a task. Rome wasn't built in a day either. Furthermore, there are also dire environmental consequences such as the extensive loss of marine habitats along the shores of Singapore. Therefore, land scarcity in Singapore will still remain as a challege in the future. Even so, Singapore has adapted to this problem well. The efficient high-rised housing system and extensive public transport system helps to alleviate the concerns of overpopulation. So while we cannot rely on land reclamation, we can always opt to expand upwards, or even downwards to help create more space for ourselves.

POPULATION CATEGORIES


There has been a consistent and constant upward trend over the years as the population of each type increases. An outlier of this significant pattern is the data from 2021, which we can attribute to the global pandemic where travel into Singapore was severly restricted. This trend indicates to us that the population of Singapore will continue to grow over the next couple of years. However, with the limited land in Singapore, the population density will keep rising, creating a potential issue of further overcrowding. This can be taxing on our public infrastructure and amenities such as the public transport system. Besides that, as housing and jobs are also scarce, competition in Singapore will get even higher, which is likely to affect social, political and economic attitudes around here.

POPULATION DENSITY

AN AGEING POPULATION

Besides the scarce land and resources in Singapore, another big challenge we face is the ageing population. Let's take a look at the latest age distribution in Singapore (using 2022 data). The histogram shows a bulk of our population between the ages 30 - 64. In fact, the age distribution is right-skewed which seems to be indicative of a healthy population since there are significantly more youths than elderly.

Hover your mouse over each bar in the histogram to see the exact number of residents within the selected age bracket. Click on the pink buttons to see the age distribution by gender.

There doesn't seem to be significant difference between the age distributions by gender. Let's scrutinise this age data and put it into a dual line graph of number of youths aged 19 & below and number of elderly aged 65 & above against time.

From 2000, the number of youths is slowly decreasing, while the number of elderly aged 65 & above is increasing steadily. This highlights the ageing problem as we have more residents aged 65 & above (who are likely to be retired) each year. As the bulk of our population is currently in the mid-range, they will soon move to the elderly range and require support from the smaller younger generation that keeps on shrinking. This would soon lead to an exhorbitant old-age dependency ratio. A higher ratio indicates more financial burden and stress on working adults and will lead to social and political instability. Therefore, our initial right-skewed data was not healthy at all, but rather already indicative of a growing ageing population.

What does the ageing population have to do with space in Singapore? Seeing that the life expectancy in Singapore is 83.7 years, it will still be quite some time before our population growth eases. Take a look at the life expectancy graph below. A noticeable trend is that the overall life expectancy in Singapore has been increasing over time. While this is good as it means our people are maintaining themselves well and staying healthier, it inadvertently contributes to a prolonged and high old-age dependency ratio.

Click on the pink buttons to view the overall life expectancy and life expectancy by gender. Click on the third button to add a new trendline of life expectancy at age 65 years to the current view.

Females have a higher life expectancy than males in Singapore. The scatter trend of "Life Expectancy at Age 65 Years" shows us how many years someone at age 65 years old has left to live. As such, it is likely that the ageing population problem will continue to persist for another 20 years minimally.

CONCLUSION

With the information we have so far, there are significant trendlines in our data that support the growing concerns on the limited space in Singapore. However, there are more factors that need to be considered and taken into account before we can deduce that there is no future left for us in Singapore. Some of such factors include governmental policies to mitigate overcrowding. According to the National Population and Talent Division, Singapore takes in a carefully controlled number of new citizens and Permanent Residents (PR) each year, to moderate the impact of low birth rates and ageing. New citizenships are selectively granted to individuals who are committed to making Singapore their home. This problem is multi-faceted and complex, and cannot be accurately answered through reviewing the data above.

The current data points towards having decreasing space left in Singapore over time, but I believe within such limited boundaries, there will still be space for us here. It is also extremely likely that our generation will receive the brunt of this problem as we will face the numerous and compounding problems that have already started or that we are even yet to know of. However, I believe that the population growth will slow and plateau eventually, and that the severity of the ageing problem will ease up after 20 years, which gives hope for the next generation after us.
